Sustainable Transport: The Future of Mobility

How shifting to low-carbon transport can reduce emissions and create healthier cities

On this page

🚲 The Problem ⚡ Electric Revolution 🚆 Public Transport 🚶 Active Travel 🌍 Student Action

The Transport Problem

Figure 1: Urban traffic congestion contributes significantly to CO₂ emissions.

Transport accounts for approximately 24% of global CO₂ emissions, with road vehicles responsible for nearly three-quarters of this figure. In cities, transport can contribute up to 40% of local air pollution, directly affecting public health. The reliance on private vehicles powered by fossil fuels is not only unsustainable but also creates congestion, noise pollution, and reduces quality of life in urban areas.

24%

of global CO₂ emissions come from transport

The good news is that solutions exist. By shifting to sustainable transport options, we can dramatically reduce emissions while creating more liveable, healthier communities.

The Electric Revolution

Figure 2: Electric vehicle charging stations are becoming increasingly common.

Electric vehicles (EVs) are transforming personal mobility. With zero tailpipe emissions, EVs eliminate local air pollution and, when charged with renewable electricity, offer a pathway to near-zero carbon transport. Battery costs have fallen by over 80% since 2010, making EVs increasingly affordable. The UK government has committed to ending the sale of new petrol and diesel cars by 2035.

Charging Infrastructure

Expanding charging networks is crucial for EV adoption. Universities and workplaces are installing charging points, making it easier for students and staff to make the switch. Many cities now offer dedicated EV parking and reduced congestion charges.

Public Transport: The Backbone of Sustainable Cities

Figure 3: Electric buses reduce emissions and improve air quality in cities.

Well-designed public transport systems can move large numbers of people efficiently and with minimal emissions. A full bus can replace up to 50 cars; a train can replace hundreds. Cities like London, Paris, and Singapore have invested heavily in expanding metro networks, bus rapid transit, and cycling infrastructure.

  • London's Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ) has reduced NO₂ emissions by 44%
  • Electric buses are now operating in over 50 UK cities
  • Student railcards offer 1/3 off train travel for young people

Active Travel: Walking and Cycling

Figure 4: Dedicated cycling infrastructure encourages active travel.

Walking and cycling produce zero emissions and offer significant health benefits. Studies show that active commuting reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease by up to 30%. Many universities are investing in secure bike storage, cycle hire schemes, and pedestrian-friendly campuses to encourage active travel.

30%

Reduced risk of heart disease with active commuting

If just 10% of short car journeys were replaced by walking or cycling, urban CO₂ emissions would drop by an estimated 5 million tonnes annually in the UK alone.

What Students Can Do

As a student, you have the power to influence transport choices on campus and beyond:

  • Choose sustainable transport - Walk, cycle, or use public transport for your daily commute
  • Use university bike schemes - Many universities offer free cycle training and bike maintenance
  • Advocate for better infrastructure - Join campaigns for safer cycle lanes and improved bus services
  • Car-share when needed - If driving is necessary, share rides to reduce emissions
  • Support campus initiatives - Get involved with sustainability committees and transport planning
