The Science of Climate Change

Understanding the mechanisms, evidence, and projections behind one of the defining challenges of the 21st century.

On this page

The Greenhouse Effect

The Earth's climate is regulated by a natural greenhouse effect. Solar radiation passes through the atmosphere and warms the planet's surface. The surface then emits infrared radiation back outward, but certain gases — known as greenhouse gases (GHGs) — absorb and re-emit this energy, trapping heat in the lower atmosphere. This process is essential for life: without it, the Earth's average temperature would be approximately −18°C.

The problem is that human activities since the Industrial Revolution have dramatically increased the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere, enhancing the greenhouse effect and causing the planet to warm beyond its natural equilibrium.

Figure 1: The enhanced greenhouse effect caused by rising CO₂ concentrations (illustrative diagram).

Key Greenhouse Gases

The main greenhouse gases driving human-caused warming are:

  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — primarily from burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and cement production. Now at 420 ppm, the highest in 800,000 years.
  • Methane (CH₄) — from agriculture (livestock, rice paddies), landfills, and natural gas leaks. About 80 times more potent than CO₂ over 20 years.
  • Nitrous oxide (N₂O) — from agricultural soils and synthetic fertilisers.
  • Fluorinated gases — industrial gases used in refrigeration and electronics, with very high warming potential.

Key Fact

Atmospheric CO₂ concentrations have increased by over 50% since pre-industrial levels. The last time CO₂ was this high was more than 3 million years ago, when sea levels were 15–25 metres higher than today.

Temperature Records

Global average temperatures have risen by approximately 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels (1850–1900 baseline). The last decade (2011–2020) was the warmest on record. Each of the last four decades has been successively warmer than any decade that preceded it since 1850.

Ice Core Evidence

Ice cores drilled in Antarctica and Greenland provide a continuous record of atmospheric composition going back 800,000 years. These records clearly show the natural cycles of glacial and interglacial periods, as well as the dramatic, unprecedented spike in greenhouse gas concentrations beginning with industrialisation.

Sea Level Rise

Global mean sea level has risen by about 20 cm since 1900, with the rate of rise accelerating. This is caused by two factors: thermal expansion of warming ocean water, and the melting of land-based ice sheets and glaciers.

1.5°C vs 2°C

The Paris Agreement set a target of limiting warming to well below 2°C, and ideally 1.5°C, above pre-industrial levels. The difference between 1.5°C and 2°C may sound small, but it represents vastly different outcomes for ecosystems, human health, food security, and the frequency of extreme weather events. At 1.5°C, coral reefs face very high risk; at 2°C, the IPCC projects a near-complete loss of tropical coral ecosystems.

The 2030 Window

To have a reasonable chance of limiting warming to 1.5°C, global greenhouse gas emissions must be cut by approximately 45% by 2030 compared to 2010 levels, and reach net zero by around 2050. Current policies put the world on track for approximately 2.5–3°C of warming.

Solutions and Technologies

Figure 4: A transition to clean energy, sustainable transport, and regenerative land use is achievable with existing technologies.

Energy System Transformation

Decarbonising the electricity sector through rapid deployment of solar, wind, and other renewables is the single most impactful lever available. Battery storage, smart grids, and demand-side management are making this transition increasingly practical and affordable.

Nature-Based Solutions

Protecting and restoring forests, wetlands, grasslands, and coastal ecosystems can provide roughly 30% of the mitigation needed by 2030 while also delivering biodiversity and water security co-benefits. These solutions are available now, often cost-effective, and have community development benefits.

Student Action

Students are uniquely positioned to accelerate climate action — through personal choices, community engagement, and professional futures.

On Campus

  • Join or establish a student climate society
  • Advocate for your university to strengthen its sustainability commitments
  • Reduce energy use in student accommodation
  • Choose plant-rich options at university canteens

In Your Future Career

Every profession will be reshaped by the transition to a low-carbon economy. Whether you enter engineering, finance, health, law, or the arts — there are opportunities to embed climate considerations into your work and contribute to systemic change.
